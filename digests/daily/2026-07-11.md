## Top Stories

### [Apple sues OpenAI over alleged trade-secret theft](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48865019)
Filed Friday in N.D. Cal, the complaint targets OpenAI's chief hardware officer Tang Tan (formerly an Apple exec for ~25 years) and former Apple materials engineer Chang Liu. Apple alleges Tan coached recruits on how to evade Apple's departure checks, including bringing actual parts to interviews "for show and tell" and using internal project codenames to extract roadmap info. Liu allegedly kept an Apple-issued laptop and exploited a vuln to download confidential docs while working at OpenAI. Tan's comms contact already scrubbed his X account. The verdict in-thread (`Robdel12`): Apple can afford to drag this through trial and a jury *loathes* AI-training-on-stolen-IP optics, so an early settlement is likely. Several comment threads drift into "Apple has done this too" (Xerox PARC, Masimo) but `JumpCrisscross` notes none of those involved a documented *exfiltration playbook*.

### [GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra produces a proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48863490)
A 3-page PDF claims an affirmative proof of a 50-year-old open problem in graph theory. The companion Lean formalization sits at `openai/cdc-lean`, and the prompt itself is published -- which is the most interesting datapoint: most of it is meta-instructions telling the model "don't stop early," "reject status reports," and "spend at least 8 hours." `ak_111` frames it as the third move beyond unit-distance-problem counterexamples and IMO proofs; the gap that remains is autonomous *theory-building* (a proof that creates 30+ pages of new machinery). `logicallee` flags that the references list goes back to 1954 with one "personal correspondence" entry -- how that was sourced is not obvious. `whitneyland` and `plaidfuji` both note this is mostly a milestone for "tasks where correctness is easily verifiable."

### [NYC bans deceptive subscription practices via Mamdani executive order](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48863464)
"Click-to-cancel" rule plus elimination of dark-pattern retention flows for billing cycles, effective immediate. `aioproductos` (a SaaS founder in-thread) confirms billing platforms ship *retention-funnel templates as the default* and you have to rip them out to be straightforward; "cancel must be as easy as signup" is the section that actually has teeth. `gumby` reminds that California already has this, and `browski` wryly notes "ironically that has meant it's hard to unsubscribe from the New York Times *except* in California" -- `aczerepinski` confirms this via the geofenced-cancel-button code he wrote for a previous employer. Wider industry question surfaces in `bkeyes`: Evernote charged the poster after "successful" cancellations and account deletion.

### [QuadRF: a hobbyist 4x4 MIMO SDR that AR-visualizes WiFi through walls](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48861717)
Jeff Geerling's build write-up of Scale RF's $99/tile (4-antenna) phased-array kit on Crowd Supply, streaming RF heat-maps into a phone camera overlay. Caveats from the creator `mrtnmcc` and the comments: it's narrowband (4.9-6 GHz, so 5 GHz WiFi only, no 2.4/BLE), has limited detection arc, and most hobby drone detection is foolhardy without extra ranges -- `fierycatnet`'s question on Ukrainian drone applicability is answered by `TrackerFF`: fiber-optic-tethered drones are RF-dark to this. Most-discussed thread in the comments: clock distribution across daisy-chained tiles. The real novelty isn't seeing WiFi through walls (always been possible) -- it's the consumer-priced phased array.

### [After 7 years in production, Scarf reluctantly moves off Haskell](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48859673)
Avi Press's long post explaining why Scarf migrated their CRUD backends from Haskell to Python despite (not because of) the language's typing strength: cold-compile times + git worktrees-per-agent adds up to ~15 minute feedback loops that don't compose with parallel coding agents. The author's `crux` summarizes the article's own thesis against itself: "fast compile times AND narrow type guardrails" exists (OCaml, Lean, Go), and the choice of Python is "driven by training-data dominance and team familiarity, not language suitability." Defenders: `cosmic_quanta` runs Haskell in production at a financial exchange and finds the same agentic workflow fine; `rkrzr` runs a shop that's deliberately split -- Haskell for platform, Python for product.
