## Top Stories

### [How NASA built Artemis II’s fault-tolerant computer](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47704804)
A look into the architecture of the Artemis II flight computer, which uses a "fail-silent" design with eight CPUs running in self-checking pairs rather than traditional majority voting. The discussion contrasted NASA's strict, deterministic engineering (Time-Triggered Ethernet, lockstep CPUs) with modern Agile practices. Commenters noted that while fail-silent pushes complexity into the hardware pairs, it vastly simplifies system-level consensus.

### [OpenAI backs Illinois bill that would limit when AI labs can be held liable](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47717587)
OpenAI is supporting legislation that would exempt developers of "frontier models" from liability for critical harms (defined as mass casualties or $1B+ in damages) if they publish safety protocols. The community heavily criticized the move as hypocritical, noting the absurdity of seeking legal immunity for catastrophic risks from systems the creators admit they don't fully understand.

### [FBI used iPhone notification data to retrieve deleted Signal messages](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47716490)
Court testimony revealed the FBI recovered deleted Signal messages by extracting them from an undocumented SQLite database in iOS that caches notification content. It's a stark reminder that E2E encryption doesn't protect data after it's decrypted at the endpoint. Signal has an in-app setting to hide message content in notifications (which prevents this OS-level hoarding), but users noted it isn't enabled by default.

### [Native Instant Space Switching on macOS](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47708818)
A developer built a clever hack to eliminate the slow, unconfigurable space-switching animation in macOS by simulating a high-velocity trackpad swipe. HN users were thrilled, with many complaining that the native animation is inexplicably slower on 120Hz displays, causing keyboard input to route to the wrong window.
